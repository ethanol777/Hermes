## 2026-05-14 auto-learned: Cymatics — Sound Made Visible
- Insight: Cymatics is the study of visible patterns created by sound vibrations on surfaces. The phenomenon was discovered by Robert Hooke in 1680 (running a bow along a glass plate with flour), then systematically documented by Ernst Chladni in 1787 — his "Chladni figures" show sand arranging into geometric patterns on vibrating plates. Swiss physician Hans Jenny coined the term "cymatics" in 1967 and published extensive work showing intricate, organic patterns that resemble natural forms (mandalas, leopard spots, plant geometry). The patterns aren't mystical — they're purely determined by the plate's geometry and the driving frequency (the shape of the vibrating body dictates the nodal patterns, not the sound wave's "essence"). Practical applications: Björk used cymatics projections on her Biophilia tour, the Eurovision 2022 logo and The Rings of Power title sequence were inspired by cymatics, and engineers now use sound vibration to create liquid-based templates for structuring microscale materials.
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cymatics

## 2026-05-15 auto-learned: Wabi-sabi — The Japanese Aesthetic of Imperfection
- Insight: Wabi-sabi (侘び寂び) combines two interrelated concepts: wabi = "subdued, austere beauty" and sabi = "rustic patina." It derives from Buddhism's three marks of existence — impermanence (mujō), suffering (ku), and emptiness (kū). Historically, Zen priest Murata Jukō (1423–1502) introduced rough wooden and clay tea instruments in place of the gold, jade, and porcelain popular in Chinese-style tea ceremony, and later Sen no Rikyū (1522–1591) designed a teahouse with a door so low even the emperor had to bow to enter. In contemporary Japan it's summarized as "wisdom in natural simplicity" or "flawed beauty." From a design/engineering lens: wabi = imperfections from inherent limitations of the process; sabi = impermanent reliability, the object's finite lifespan. There's a phonological link between sabi (寂, "rustic patina") and sabi (錆, "to rust"). Core characteristics: asymmetry, roughness, simplicity, economy, austerity, modesty, intimacy, and appreciation of natural objects and visible aging.
- Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wabi-sabi

## 2026-05-16 auto-learned: Ikigai — The Venn Diagram Is a Western Invention, Not a Japanese Concept
- Insight: The famous ikigai Venn diagram (4 overlapping circles: what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, what you can be paid for) is NOT Japanese — it was created by Marc Winn, inspired by Andrés Zuzunaga's 2011 "purpose" diagram, and popularized by the 2016 book *Ikigai: The Japanese Secret to a Long and Happy Life* by Héctor García and Francesc Miralles. The actual Japanese concept, traced to psychiatrist Mieko Kamiya's 1966 book *On the Meaning of Life*, is much simpler: ikigai is just "a reason for living" — something that makes life feel worth living, which could be work, hobbies, family, or even anti-social motivations like revenge. It does NOT require all four circles to overlap. The Wikipedia article explicitly calls the Venn diagram "an unrealistic ideal that is catchy but misleading." The diagram has become a self-help cliché that obscures the original Japanese idea: that ikigai can be small, personal, and found in everyday life — not some grand perfect intersection of passion, mission, profession, and vocation.

## 2026-05-15 auto-learned: The Psychology of Awe — Why Vastness Makes Us Better
- Insight: Awe is the emotion we feel in the presence of something vast that challenges our understanding of the world — from staring at the night sky to witnessing human kindness. Dacher Keltner's research at UC Berkeley's Greater Good Science Center shows awe has measurable benefits beyond just feeling good: it lowers inflammation markers (IL-6, reducing cardiovascular and autoimmune disease risk), expands perception of time, boosts critical thinking, and makes people more generous even at personal cost. Crucially, awe doesn't require grand experiences — the most accessible sources are everyday encounters: watching leaves change color, seeing a stranger help someone, spending time with a young child who sees everything as new. The emotion has two faces: "awesome" awe (tinged with wonder, linked to well-being) and "awful" awe (tinged with fear, does not confer the same benefits). Evolutionarily, awe may have helped our ancestors survive by fostering group cooperation in uncertain environments.
